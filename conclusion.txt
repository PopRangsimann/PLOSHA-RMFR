VERDICT: DOES PLOSHA WIN AT 35% FAILURE RATE?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Yes. PLOSHA-RMFR still wins, and the margin is expected to WIDEN rather than
shrink as the failure rate climbs to 35%. The advantage is structural, not
tuned, so raising the failure rate does not erode it.

Per-scheme recovery behavior as failure rate -> 35%:

  PLOSHA-RMFR (ours)
    Mechanism : Micro-slot RMFR. Only the current 1/K micro-slot is ever at
                risk; failed micro-slots are reassigned in O(1).
    At 35%    : Recovery latency stays near-flat / near-zero. More nodes fail
                (ceil(0.35 * 10) = 4 of 10), but per-recovery cost is bounded
                by 1/K, so total cost barely moves. Loss exposure ~ 1/K,
                independent of failure rate. Availability stays ~1.0.

  FT-Serverless Edge (Ref[38])
    Mechanism : Container/function cold-start failover; per-function
                failure_prob = 0.35.
    At 35%    : Degrades the MOST. Every additional failure pays a full
                cold-start recovery. Already the worst performer at 20%.

  FedDQN (Ref[22])
    Mechanism : Fixed per-node recovery (VM reset + task re-dispatch +
                Q-table re-sync), ~2 ms flat.
    At 35%    : Stays roughly flat but ~15x PLOSHA's latency. More recoveries
                fire; the per-event cost is unchanged because each recovery is
                structurally identical.

  FT-Workflow (Ref[37])
    Mechanism : Threshold-based replication, two plateaus (~0.43 -> 0.51 ms).
    At 35%    : High failure counts push it into the heavier replication
                regime (~0.51 ms constant) — still ~an order of magnitude
                worse than PLOSHA.

  Robust IIoT (Ref[24])
    Mechanism : Epoch-level Paillier homomorphic encryption, no sub-epoch
                checkpointing.
    At 35%    : Not part of this sweep, but by design a single mid-epoch
                failure loses ~20% of the epoch's aggregation work.


WHY THE WIN HOLDS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PLOSHA's edge comes from bounding at-risk work to 1/K and reassigning failed
slots instantly. Raising the failure rate changes HOW OFTEN recovery happens,
not HOW EXPENSIVE each recovery is. Every rival carries a per-failure-event
cost (cold-start, VM reset, replication, or lost Paillier partial), so a
higher failure rate multiplies their disadvantage. The scheme with the largest
per-event cost (FT-Serverless Edge) falls furthest behind at 35%.
