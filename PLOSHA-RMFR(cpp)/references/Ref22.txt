ABSTRACT Task scheduling in distributed cloud and fog computing applications must be efficient to
optimize resource utilization, minimize latency, and comply with strict service level agreements. The
dynamic and heterogeneous of fog computing challenges such as balancing performance with variable task
loads, resource constraints, and energy efficiency. This paper introduces a new Federated Learning Deep
Queue-Learning (FLDQN) framework that integrates reinforcement learning with federated learning to
overcome these challenges. The FLDQN approach enables decentralized task scheduling because fog nodes
can train their local DQN models on localized data, merging their experiences to form a global model without
sensitive data sharing. It further improves task prioritization by classifying tasks based on their execution time
and deadlines, ensuring that high-priority tasks are scheduled first, which reduces SLA violations and task
rejection rates. The proposed model dynamically adapts to the changing conditions of the fog environment
through iterative learning that continuously improves work allocation. Substantial experiments conducted
on large and small sizes of dataset show that the proposed FLDQN outperforms others, including standalone
DQN and graph-based GGCN. For all dataset sizes small, medium, and large datasets, it reduces makespan
by up to 30%, improves throughput, and reduces energy by distributing tasks on the most efficient node based
on current system states. The results demonstrate the ability of FLDQN to optimize task execution in real
time, while addressing the scalability and privacy issues inherent in fog computing. Combining federated
and reinforcement learning proposed framework provides a flexible solution for distributed task scheduling,
which is well suited for latency-sensitive applications in industrial automation, healthcare monitoring, and
smart city deployments.

distances, which can cause delays that affect real-time appli-

A. BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
The rapid growth of Internet of Things (IoT) applications,
smart cities, and edge-centric services has increased the
need for low-latency, efficient, and scalable computing
solutions. Cloud-based infrastructures based on centralized
processing fail to meet these needs due to high network
latency, bandwidth constraints, and centralized processing
limitations. As a result, fog computing has emerged as a
potential paradigm, bringing cloud capabilities to the edge
through localized computation, low latency, and improved
system responsiveness. However, the resource-constrained
and decentralized nature of fog environments introduces
serious challenges in task scheduling, resource management,
and energy efficiency. Real-time task scheduling is a major
challenge in fog computing, which involves making smart
decisions for dynamic allocation of computational resources
according to service level agreements (SLAs) and minimizing
makespan, throughput, and energy consumption.
Heuristic-based scheduling methods, while being optimal
in static settings, do not cope well with the highly dynamic
and volatile nature of fog workloads. This calls for the use
of learning-based methods that can continuously improve
scheduling policies in line with real-time system evolution.
To mitigate this, federated learning (FL) and deep rein-
forcement learning (DRL) have proven to be revolutionary
solutions. FL facilitates decentralized model training on
fog nodes, enabling them to learn scheduling policies in a
collaborative manner without exchanging raw data, thereby
preserving data confidentiality and reducing communication

II. RELATED WORK
In [11], the author proposes a new approach to task
scheduling optimization targeting IoT systems with cloud-
fog computing. An advanced hybrid algorithm AEOSSA
proposed that integrates the advantages of AEO and SSA.
This synergistic merger solves the intrinsic problems of
AEO due to AEO’s location trapping, which is weakly
exploited due to its weak exploitation ability. This makes
AEOSSA efficient and more effective in solving scheduling
problems. AEOSSA also defines the makespan, throughput
and the overall performance of optimize for these critical
metrics. To demonstrate its utility, the authors set up their
experiments on a very wide range of synthetic and real data
sets, namely NASA Ames iPSC/860 and HPC2N workloads.
The experimental results prove that AEOSSA outperforms all
other competing conventional state-of-the-art metaheuristic
algorithms, such as PSO, Harris Hawkes Optimization
(HHO), and Firefly Algorithm (FA). The proposed algorithm
has better convergence time and higher throughput with less
makespan compared to other tasks.
In [12], a novel algorithm named PQFAHP (Priority
Queue Fuzzy Analysis Hierarchical Process) is designed
for task-scheduling in a mobile fog computing (MFC).
Fuzzy logic and Analytic Hierarchy Process are integrated
to base the coordinated scheduling of tasks on several
criteria such as completion time, energy consumption,
RAM and deadline. It prioritizes MFC tasks and optimizes
resource allocation for fast execution with minimized energy
consumption. The study also raises awareness that MFC can
be used as a medium to get resource-intensive applications
to mobile devices with very low energy resources. The
essence of PQFAHP is to provide dynamic scheduling of
deadline-sensitive tasks using multiple parameters to deter-
mine task priorities. PQFAHP is proven to improvements
in waiting time, delay, SLA, average response time and
task scheduling efficiency compared to traditional scheduling
algorithms such as FCFS, min-heap and max-heap.
In author’s [13], a multi-objective scheduling scheme is

Scheduling Algorithm (CAMOSA) solves task scheduling
problems across heterogeneous fog nodes with different
resource capabilities and user requirements. CAMOSA relies
on metaheuristics for optimization to achieve trade-offs
between conflicting objectives. The main results of the

existing approaches in terms of execution cost, task comple-

conditions. This makes it possible to adapt CAMOSA to
different resource configurations that dynamically change
with user needs, thereby strengthening the applicability of
this algorithm to real-world fog computing deployments. This
study has limitations, such as it does not scale in larger and
more complex fog infrastructures and requires fine-tuning of
algorithm parameters for different environments.

IV. PROPOSED TASK SCHEDULING USING FEDERATED
DQN
Task scheduling in the distributed fog environment is critical
in optimizing resource allocation, which lowers latency
and will result in a smooth operation of complex systems.
Considering the edge devices are sending tens of thousands
of tasks to the incoming cloud, smart adaptive and efficient
mechanisms of scheduling are mandatory. Unlike traditional
cloud environments, fog environments bring decentraliza-
tion and diverse resource pools, which, in turn, introduce
proximity near the edge, thus favoring low latency but
necessitating more dynamic resource management in order to
avoid bottlenecks while maximizing throughput. Therefore,
it is hard to allocate the computational tasks for virtual
machines such that the performance of the system, energy
efficiency, and SLA are all satisfied. The ultimate aim is to
handle tasks as quickly as possible without wasting resources,
minimizing task rejections, and avoiding SLA violations.
Federated Deep Queue-Learning (DQN) is the revolutionary
solution to the task scheduling challenges of distributed
fog environments. It relies on reinforcement learning (RL)
to introduce an intelligent, self-optimizing approach that
spreads learning across individual fog nodes. Each node
runs in a local DQN agent constantly learning the policy
of optimal assignment of tasks locally and based upon the
localized state of the environmental conditions. Since this is
what makes localized learning possible, localized learning
allows node operation with highly autonomous behavior
compared to centralized ones. Decentralization is found to be
essential in large scale fog networks so that system scalability
increased and communication overhead reduced, along with
latency-related delays and poor synchronization.
A. FLDQN FRAMEWORK
The FLDQN system architecture Fig:2 is based on optimizing
the scheduling of tasks in fog computing environments
using deep reinforcement learning and federated learning
principles. It starts from a representation of system states,
which captures real-time information about fog nodes,
including task queue lengths and resource availability. Once
a task scheduling decision is made, it moves to the next
state according to the updated conditions. The actions in
this architecture correspond to selecting specific virtual
machines (VMs) or resources for task execution. The impact
of these decisions is assessed through a reward mechanism.
To improve decision-making, a replay memory buffer is used
to store past experiences that include states, actions, rewards,
and next states. These learned experiences are used to train
a deep reinforcement learning model. It is also known as a
Q-network. It estimates action-value functions, such as Q-
values, to nudge the system toward optimal task allocation.
Then, a global model is built by integrating the models trained
from individual fog nodes on a federated learning server. This
global model, which captures the collective knowledge from
all nodes, is distributed back to the fog nodes while preserving
privacy and optimizing performance.
Deep Q-Networks (DQN) have emerged as a powerful
tool for solving complex reinforcement learning problems,
particularly in areas like task scheduling in cloud and
fog computing environments. Task scheduling is a critical
challenge in these environments, where resources must
be allocated efficiently to meet stringent Service Level

C. STATE
In Federated Deep Queue-Networks (FLDQN) for task
scheduling, state representation plays a key role in making
informed decisions based on local conditions at each fog node
while contributing to the global learning process. The state
vector s captures key information about the environment,
including the number of virtual machines (VMs), tasks to
be scheduled, and resource utilization at each fog node. This
representation allows the agent to assess the current system
state and make decisions that lead to optimal task allocation
and efficient resource management.
The state vector s consists of several components. The
first component, s1, is the current queue length of each VM.
It indicates how many tasks are waiting to be processed at
that node. The queue length is an important factor because
it helps the agent evaluate whether the VM is overloaded or
underutilized. It is defined as:

whether the available VMs have enough computational

can be expressed as:
s2 = CPU requirement of taskj
(23)

priority, denoted by s3. Task priority denotes the urgency
associated with each task and helps the agent prioritize tasks
based on their higher priority over other tasks. This is encoded
by a numerical value: 0 for low, 1 for medium, and 2 for high.

D. ACTION
In Federated Deep Queue-Networks (FLDQN) for task
scheduling, the set of possible actions an agent may take in
any given step at the time of a decision point is presented
by the action space A. Here an action would define selecting
a fog node virtual machine on which some task needs to be
assigned so as to run on that selected node. The action space
is dynamic as it depends on the number of VMs available
in the system, and the agent must select the optimal VM
based on the current state of the system, which includes
task requirements, VM queue lengths, and task priorities.
The agent evaluates the VMs available in the system at each
step and selects the one that maximizes the expected future
reward. Q-values are used for action selection and represent
the long-term reward associated with taking a particular
action in a state. The VM chosen by the agent is that action
a∗having the highest Q-value for the current state s:
a∗= arg max

that estimates the expected future reward for taking action a
in state s. Once an action a∗is selected, the task is assigned to
the selected VM and the system transitions to a new state s′
that reflects the updated system conditions, such as VM queue
lengths, resource utilization, and task completion statuses.
The agent receives a reward based on the effectiveness of the
action in meeting task scheduling goals (e.g., task completion
time, SLA violations, energy consumption, or throughput).
In FLDQN, this action selection process is performed
locally at each fog node, where the node’s local DQN model
predicts possible actions based on the local state. However,
the agent’s decisions and Q-values are aggregated over time
with other fog nodes to improve the global model, allowing
the entire system to benefit from shared knowledge and
collectively optimize the task scheduling strategy.

nodes in the federated learning scheme. The selecting actions
that maximize expected Q-values, the FLDQN agent aims
to optimize resource utilization, reduce task rejection rates,
and ensure that tasks in the fog network meet their respective
deadlines and performance goals.

F. HYPER PARAMETER
The network architecture consists of an input layer, hidden
layers and output layers. The input layer is responsi-
ble for encoding system states to fully understand the
model workload conditions. The hidden layers consist of
two fully connected layers with 128 and 64 neurons,
respectively, applying the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)
activation function to improve learning efficiency and avoid
vanishing gradient problems. The output layer generates
Q-values for various scheduling actions, using a Softmax
activation function for probabilistic selection of actions.
FLDQN architecture supports its ability to iteratively learn
its scheduling approach, optimizing makespan, energy effi-
ciency, and system throughput as it dynamically adapts
to workload fluctuations. FLDQN success is fueled by a
well-calibrated set of hyperparameters that stabilize learning
and sharpen decision-making accuracy. The learning rate,
α
= 0.001, facilitates smooth but sensitive adjustments
to new scheduling contexts, avoiding wild oscillations in
Q-value updates. The discount factor, γ = 0.95, balances
short-term efficiency and long-term scheduling effectiveness,
ensuring task assignments maximize immediate rewards
while contributing to overall system stability. To ensure
efficient training and computational efficiency, a batch size
of 32 is used, allowing the model to learn from its parameters
based on a representative sample of previous experiences.
FLDQN also employs an ϵ-gradient search mechanism,
where ϵ starts at 1.0 and gradually decreases, enabling the
model to explore various scheduling options before settling
on the optimal policy.

1) COMPUTATIONAL OVERHEAD OF FLDQN
The computational burden of FLDQN consists of two primary
components: the cost of training Deep Q-Network (DQN)
models at each fog node and the communication burden
imposed by federated learning. Each fog node trains a
local DQN model to learn task scheduling options, thereby
improving processing loads. Our evaluation demonstrates
that a single iteration of the DQN model requires 12.8 ms
and 48.6 MB of memory per fog node. This translates
to an additional 19% in computational time over the
standalone DQN method. However, the increased complexity
contributes to better scheduling accuracy, task rejection rates
are reduced by 27% and SLA violations by 22%, resulting
in better resource utilization. Federated learning imposes a
communication overhead, in which each node periodically
sends its model weights obtained from training to the global
server. Each update cycle sends approximately 152 KB of
data to the node every five scheduling periods. This increases
network bandwidth utilization by 6.7%, yet the improved
global decision making greatly reduces overall task latency.
The improved task allocation yields a 30% lower makespan
and an 18% increased throughput, which shows an overhead
vs. performance In addition to increasing training complexity,
FLDQN ensures real-time task scheduling efficiency by
optimizing update rates and reducing unnecessary model
synchronizations. The training frequency is dynamically
adjusted to balance the computational load while keeping
the schedulability response intact. Our research reveals that
FLDQN achieves an average scheduling efficiency of 92.4%
across various workload scenarios. Our results prove that
despite the high computational and communication overhead,
the improved task scheduling capability justifies the overhead
as an effective solution for real-time fog computing.
FLDQN is designed to maximize task scheduling and
reduce overall energy consumption, it also adds additional
energy overhead as it trains and updates DQN models on
each fog node. To measure this, we compare the energy
consumption of FLDQN in two ways energy used to train
local DQN model and energy used during federated learning
model aggregation and updates. Our findings indicate that
each training iteration on a fog node consumes approximately
2.3 joules, while local DQN updates are executed every five
scheduling periods. Federated learning model aggregation
consumes an additional 1.1 joules per update, which is mainly
due to communication overhead. FLDQN still maintains an
overall reduction of 17.4% compared to single DQN and
heuristic-based scheduling methods. This is because FLDQN
optimally distributes tasks to energy-efficient nodes, avoiding
unnecessary task migrations and overloading high-energy
consuming devices.

example, when there are insufficient resources (e.g., CPU,

evaluating task scheduling algorithms, because the lower the

VI. CONCLUSION
This paper presents a comprehensive task scheduling frame-
work for distributed fog computing using Federated Deep
Queue-Learning (FLDQN). By federating and combin-
ing reinforcement learning, FLDQN enables decentralized,
75490
VOLUME 13, 2025
P. Choppara, S. S. Mangalampalli: Adaptive Task Scheduling in Fog Computing Using Federated DQN
adaptive scheduling to overcome issues such as latency,
resource heterogeneity, and SLA compliance. The model
improves task allocation through localized learning at fog
nodes, thereby eliminating bottlenecks and optimizing per-
formance. The main contribution here is the use of K-means
clustering for task ordering in terms of urgency and required
resources. Only critical tasks are processed first, so SLA vio-
lations as well as task rejection rates are reduced. Federated
learning updates local models cooperatively, ensuring that
data confidentiality is maintained with minimal communi-
cation overhead. Experimental results FLDQN outperforms
DQN and GGCN across all dataset sizes. FLDQN achieves
a 25-35% makespan improvement for small datasets of
100-350 tasks, a 28% improvement for medium datasets of
400-650 tasks, and up to 30% improvement for large datasets
of 700-1000 tasks. In addition to makespan reduction,
FLDQN reduces SLA violations by 22-30% and task
rejection rate by 20-27%, ensuring more tasks are completed
within deadline constraints. Distributing workloads across
fog nodes to improve system performance increases model
throughput by 18-25%. It also contributes to energy effi-
ciency by reducing consumption by 15-20% by dynamically
assigning tasks to less loaded and more energy-efficient
nodes. FLDQN improves throughput, reduces energy con-
sumption, and efficiently distributes workloads across fog
nodes, thereby demonstrating scalability for real-time appli-
cations such as smart cities and industrial automation.
Advanced methods for clustering, multi-objective optimiza-
tion, dynamic scaling of resources, real-world deployment,
and hybrid models of learning. All these advances will
improve the adaptability and efficiency of FLDQN in
distributed environments for scalable and privacy-preserving
task scheduling in the future.
